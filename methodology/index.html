Methodology

How we grade cannabis claims

A transparent, reproducible framework for evaluating claims against published evidence.

Evaluation process

Each claim passes through a five-step evaluation:

  1. Claim extraction: Identify the precise, falsifiable claim from the source material (news article, social media post, product marketing, or public statement).
  2. Literature search: Systematic search of PubMed, Cochrane Library, and clinical trial registries for relevant published evidence.
  3. Source appraisal: Grade each source by study design, sample size, replication status, and conflict-of-interest disclosure.
  4. Evidence synthesis: Weigh the body of evidence for consistency, effect size, and external validity.
  5. Grade assignment: Assign a letter grade and confidence level.

Grading rubric

GradeLabelCriteria
AStrong evidenceMultiple high-quality RCTs or systematic reviews with consistent, statistically significant results. Low risk of bias. Replicated across independent research groups.
BModerate evidenceAt least one well-conducted RCT or multiple consistent observational studies. Some heterogeneity in methods or populations.
CLimited evidenceObservational studies, case series, or preclinical data only. No controlled human trials, or trials with significant methodological limitations.
DInsufficient evidenceConflicting studies with no clear signal. No controlled data, or only anecdotal reports. Insufficient evidence to support or refute the claim.
FContradictedMultiple well-conducted studies refute the claim. Strong evidence against it.

Confidence levels

Each grade is accompanied by a confidence percentage reflecting our certainty that additional evidence would not change the grade:

  • High confidence (≥80%): Large, consistent body of evidence; unlikely to change with new studies.
  • Moderate confidence (50–79%): Adequate evidence with some uncertainty; new studies could refine the grade.
  • Low confidence (<50%): Sparse or conflicting evidence; grade is provisional and likely to change.

Source hierarchy

Evidence is weighted by study design, from strongest to weakest:

  1. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs
  2. Individual randomized controlled trials
  3. Controlled observational studies (cohort, case-control)
  4. Uncontrolled observational studies and case series
  5. Preclinical studies (animal, in vitro)
  6. Expert opinion and anecdotal reports

Correction policy

When new evidence changes a claim's grade, we publish a dated correction on the claim record and in the corrections log. Subscribers to the evidence newsletter receive correction notices. We do not silently update grades.

Limitations

  • Cannabis research is constrained by federal scheduling in the US, limiting the quantity and quality of available RCTs.
  • Product variability (strain, potency, delivery method) complicates generalization.
  • Publication bias may overrepresent positive findings.
  • Evidence grades reflect published research, not clinical practice guidelines.
